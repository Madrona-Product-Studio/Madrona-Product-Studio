# Packaging, Pricing & Process: How Best-in-Class Product Studios Sell Their Work — and What Madrona Product Studio Should Borrow

## TL;DR
- **The single most copyable pattern is ustwo's four-named-package framework ("Discover / Shape / 0 to 1 / Boost") with explicit "From X weeks" durations and Activities/Outcomes/Timeframe blocks on each sub-page** — it lets a small studio look productized without publishing a price list, and it maps cleanly to Madrona's "decide what to build, then make it real" positioning.
- **Almost no peer studio publishes prices**, so the bar for transparency is low: even a public "typical engagement starts at $X" or a Greater Good Studio–style "Number of Hours × Hourly Rate" formula on a Working With Us page would be more transparent than ustwo, frog, IDEO, Metalab, Substantial, Artefact, or Funsize. Useful directional anchors: a secondary citation on the proto.io blog reports "One Google Ventures Design Partner, Daniel Burka, recommended facilitators charge between $9,000–15,000 to hold sprints" (treat as second-hand, not a primary Burka publication); Greater Good Studio publicly states a "typical GGS project is 3-6 months"; Zypsy publicly anchors a 5/10-day/6–8-week sprint ladder with Webflow projects "starting at $60,000" and a "~$100k for ~1% SAFE" equity track.
- **Mission-driven positioning works best when it's structural, not decorative** — Greater Good Studio is a for-profit social enterprise that helps clients co-write grant proposals, IDEO.org is a 501(c)(3) that asks partners to cover direct costs while it fundraises indirect costs, and Wholegrain Digital is a B Corp whose B Lab Global directory listing confirms "Wholegrain Digital earned an overall score of 122.1. The median score for ordinary businesses who complete the assessment is currently 50.9." Madrona's "studio for good" claim should be backed by at least one similarly concrete mechanism.

## Key Findings

**1. The dominant packaging pattern is 3–4 named, sequential phases with stated durations and no published price.** ustwo, frog, IDEO.org, Substantial, and the UK GDS Discovery–Alpha–Beta–Live cadence all use a phase model. ustwo is the cleanest version: four named packages — Discover, Shape, 0 to 1, Boost — each on its own sub-page with a stated Timeframe ("From 3 months" for Shape; "From 6 weeks to 1 year" for 0 to 1; "From 6 weeks" for Boost) and Key Activities / Example Outcomes blocks. Substantial publishes a parallel set of named offerings — Strategy Sprints (2–4 weeks: "In two to four weeks, our team embeds with yours to map how your business actually operates"), Build Sprints (4 weeks: "Working software in four weeks—built against your real workflows, with your real data"), Deploy, Operate, and Strategy Advisory — with concrete outcomes per phase. frog uses Discover → Design → Deliver. IDEO.org uses two umbrella verbs — "Listening, inviting, learning, noticing, and sense-making" → "Imagining, building, co-creating, and testing." None publish prices.

**2. Sprints are the most productized line item in the industry.** Jake Knapp's 5-day GV sprint anchors the format; AJ&Smart built an entire training business around facilitating it (the Design Sprint Masterclass is currently listed as closed on both facilitator.com and aj-smart.teachable.com — "The Design Sprint Masterclass is currently closed. Sign up to the waitlist" — and no current public price is displayed); thoughtbot publishes a free 5-day Sprint Guide and offers paid sprint engagements; Zypsy productizes a three-tier ladder (5-Day, 10-Day, 6–8 Week). Public price points: Zypsy's Webflow Premium Partner profile lists projects "starting at $60,000"; Zypsy's "Design Capital" trades 8–10 weeks of work for ~1% SAFE equity valued up to ~$100K. Daniel Burka's much-cited GV facilitator benchmark of $9,000–$15,000 per sprint comes via a proto.io secondary citation, not a primary Burka publication, so use it directionally.

**3. Embedded squads / staff-augmentation is the second productized line.** ustwo's "Boost" is explicitly "plug-and-play integrated, scaleable teams… to increase momentum, expand and enhance ideas, and improve ways of working." Substantial built its career on integrated "Engagement Manager + design + dev" pods. Metalab works as an embedded co-founder team — and now invests via Metalab Ventures, which TechCrunch (Christine Hall, April 9, 2024) reports "has raised $15 million in capital commitments for its first fund to invest in product-led startups where strategy, design and technology are the key differentiators… Des Cotes and David Tapp… are the general partners at Metalab Ventures and will invest in 25 to 35 startups at the pre-seed, seed and Series A stages." Thoughtbot, Funsize, and others all run hybrid project + retainer.

**4. Equity-for-services is the most experimental but emerging compensation model among small-to-mid studios.** Metalab Ventures (per TechCrunch, April 2024) formalized this with a $15M fund. Zypsy's Design Capital is the cleanest public spec: 8–10 weeks for ~1% SAFE up to ~$100K of value. ustwo historically built and spun out its own products (Monument Valley, Moodnotes) rather than taking client equity. This is a positioning lever a senior studio can pull selectively.

**5. Mission-driven studios use one of three financial structures, and they say so explicitly.**
- **For-profit social enterprise** (Greater Good Studio): "We are a social enterprise, which is a for-profit, mission-based business" — they choose clients whose mission "will advance a more equitable society," charge "Number of Hours multiplied by Hourly Rate," and are happy to support funding through "co-writing grant proposals, creating pitch presentations and joining for meetings with funders." Typical project: 3–6 months.
- **501(c)(3) cross-subsidy** (IDEO.org): "We ask the nonprofit partners we work with to pay the direct costs of our work with them. And we raise money through fundraising to cover our indirect costs." This lets them "select the projects we undertake based on their potential for impact, rather than an organization's ability to pay."
- **Certified B Corp + measurable impact metric** (Wholegrain Digital): B Impact score 122.1 vs. a 50.9 median (per B Lab Global's directory). Wholegrain's own About page commits: "To ensure that the average CO2/page view of all new websites is less than the industry average (currently 0.5 grams per page view)" — i.e., 0.5g/pageview is the threshold, not the older 1.76g industry average commonly cited.

**6. The "How we work" page that converts best is concrete, weekly, and visual.** Greater Good Studio's Working With Us page directly states project length, the formula they price by, what "fit" looks like, and answers "Can you help us find the funding to work with you?" with a yes plus co-written grant proposals. Substantial's homepage uses an expandable "+" pattern with five offerings, each listing 3–4 bulleted Outcomes. ustwo's package pages each have "Key activities / Example outcomes / Timeframe" as a structured spec block. thoughtbot publishes a public day-by-day Sprint Guide site (design.thoughtbot.com/sprint-guide) so prospects can self-educate before contacting them. None of the aspirational-tier firms (IDEO, frog, fjord/Accenture Song) publish process pages this concrete — that's a gap a senior small studio can exploit.

**7. The single most differentiated small-studio voice is Big Medium's.** Brad Frost / Josh Clark's Big Medium positions purely on craft + relationships ("success has far less to do with assets and more to do with people and their relationships to one another… working with humans is Big Medium's secret sauce") and on a focused vertical (enterprise design systems). They don't try to do everything; they do one thing for "the world's biggest companies." This is the model Madrona should study most closely as a senior, founder-led shop — except substituting their own focus area (decide what to build → make it real, with a health/outdoor-stewardship lean).

### Specific URLs worth studying directly

- **ustwo "What we do" hub + four sub-pages** (`/what-we-do/discover/`, `/shape/`, `/0-to-1/`, `/boost/`) — gold-standard productization template; each page has Key activities / Example outcomes / Timeframe.
- **Substantial.com homepage** — five named offerings each in an expandable "+" with 3–4 bulleted Outcomes per offering; "Strategy Sprints" sub-page also worth modeling.
- **Greater Good Studio "Working With Us"** (greatergoodstudio.com/working-with-us/) — best-in-class FAQ-style page covering client fit, project length, pricing formula, grant-writing support, and "Are you a nonprofit?" answered transparently.
- **thoughtbot Sprint Guide** (design.thoughtbot.com/sprint-guide/) — a free public micro-site teaching the methodology, with a soft "Hire us" CTA. Strongest "demonstrate value before the call" play in the industry.
- **Big Medium homepage + Brad Frost's "Joining Big Medium" announcement post** — model of small-senior-studio voice and explicit "we are a team, not a person" positioning.
- **IDEO.org Services page** (ideo.org/services) — five named pillars (Codesign, Community Engagement, Futuring, Narrative Strategy, etc.) each with one case study link; concise.
- **Wholegrain Digital "Digital Sustainability"** (wholegraindigital.com/digital-sustainability/) — model of mission-driven productization (Carbon Audit as a discrete offering).
- **ustwo case studies tagged by package** (e.g., Sproutiful — "Making behavioural change simple, from zero to working product in 8 weeks" as the headline) — model of pulling the engagement-shape story up into the case-study title itself.

### Patterns the best examples share

1. **Name the package, then show the math.** ustwo, Substantial, IDEO.org, frog all give offerings memorable names. Then they show timeframes, deliverables, and outcomes in scannable blocks.
2. **Sprints are entry points, not products.** Studios that do sprints almost always frame them as the start of a longer arc — thoughtbot's sprint leads to build; Substantial's Strategy Sprint "carries straight into building"; Zypsy's 5/10-day cascade up to 6–8 weeks. Sprints sell the relationship.
3. **One sentence per case study explains the engagement shape.** ustwo's tags ("Idea to launch, validating a business proposition in market"; "Roadmapping a new notification system"; "from zero to working product in 8 weeks") tell prospects "this is a project like yours" before they read a word.
4. **Outcomes language beats output language.** Substantial: "Cut B2B sales cycles by 63% with a conversational AI agent." Substantial: "1,200 minutes replaced." Greater Good Studio quotes are about transformation ("see ourselves clearly… dream big") rather than artifacts.
5. **The "what we don't do" is implicit but visible.** Big Medium does design systems for big companies; they don't do brand identity. Wholegrain does WordPress sustainability; they don't do mobile apps. Greater Good does social-sector HCD; they don't do startup pitch decks. Senior small studios are remembered for what they refuse.
6. **Mission claims are backed by structural choices.** Either a 501(c)(3) status (IDEO.org), a B Corp certification with a measurable impact metric (Wholegrain Digital, B Impact 122.1), a social-enterprise charter with a stated client filter (Greater Good Studio), or an explicit "we co-write grants with you" service. Vague mission language without a structural mechanism reads as marketing.

### Gaps / opportunities almost no one does well

1. **Public price ranges.** Even a "typical engagement starts at $X" or "we work with budgets from $Y to $Z" — like Zypsy's $60K Webflow floor or Hex Studio's "$15–30K USD, 6–12 weeks" full-cycle brand projects, or Hex's "$8k/month" open-ended retainer. Almost no senior studio does this; clients (especially mission-driven ones with limited budgets) self-select before contacting you.
2. **A visual week-by-week or phase-by-phase artifact.** Most studios describe phases in prose. Almost none publish a sample one-page calendar showing what Week 1, Week 2, Week 3 actually look like. ustwo's case studies show this implicitly; nobody shows it explicitly on a service page.
3. **Sample deliverable artifacts shown publicly.** thoughtbot links to a free Sprint Guide; Wholegrain shares its Website Carbon rating system. But no one publishes a sample synthesis deck, sample roadmap page, sample prototype-test summary as a "this is what you'll receive" gallery.
4. **Explicit mission-aligned offerings with a separate path.** IDEO.org and Greater Good and Dalberg Design are entire firms dedicated to mission work. Almost no commercial product studio publishes a dedicated mission-aligned engagement (e.g., "Stewardship Sprint: 4 weeks at our nonprofit rate, includes pitch deck and grant-narrative support"). This is a wide-open positioning lane.
5. **Founder-led senior-collaborator model named explicitly.** Big Medium does this best ("we've historically presented ourselves as a loose collection of individuals, which isn't how we've operated in years… So we're addressing that by explicitly flying under a single flag"). Madrona has a chance to flip the typical agency framing — "you're hiring a senior team that's small on purpose, not small because it's starting out."

## Recommendations

### What Madrona should borrow

**Stage 1 (do now):**
- **Adopt ustwo's four-package structure as a template, but rename for Madrona's positioning.** Suggested set:
  - **"Decide" (Discover analog)** — 2–4 weeks. Activities: stakeholder interviews, opportunity mapping, problem framing, "what to build" recommendation. Outcome: a one-page strategic brief + prioritized roadmap.
  - **"Prototype" (Shape analog)** — 4–6 weeks. Activities: rapid prototyping, user testing, hypothesis-driven validation. Outcome: testable prototype + research synthesis + go/no-go recommendation.
  - **"Build" (0 to 1 analog)** — 8–16 weeks. Activities: production design + build + launch readiness. Outcome: shipped MVP + handoff documentation.
  - **"Embedded" (Boost analog)** — ongoing, monthly retainer. Activities: senior fractional product/design leadership. Outcome: capability and momentum, not artifacts.
- **For each package, publish a Key Activities / Outcomes / Timeframe spec block** — exactly the ustwo template. Three lines, scannable.
- **Tag case studies by package.** Pull the engagement shape into the title (e.g., "Decide Sprint: Re-framing the membership model for a regional land trust, in 4 weeks").

**Stage 2 (next 60–90 days):**
- **Publish a Working With Us page modeled on Greater Good Studio's**, including: (a) typical project length, (b) pricing formula even if not full prices ("Hours × Rate, with a typical project ranging $X–$Y"), (c) the explicit "Are you a mission-driven org? Here's our path for you" answer, (d) "We will co-write your grant" if Madrona is willing.
- **Define the mission-aligned engagement explicitly.** Suggested: "Stewardship Sprint" — a discrete 2–4-week Decide-package offering at a published rate (e.g., 50% of standard) for qualifying health/wellness/outdoor-stewardship organizations, with a stated cap (e.g., "we run 4 of these per year"). Greater Good Studio's "social enterprise" framing is the cleanest reference; Big Medium's no-bullshit voice is the tonal reference.
- **Pick one structural mechanism that backs the "studio for good" claim.** Options, ranked by lift: (1) become a B Corp; (2) commit a fixed % of capacity (e.g., 20%) to mission-aligned work and publish a year-end impact report; (3) co-write grants with mission clients à la Greater Good; (4) sliding-scale rate card by org size. Pick one and operationalize it; don't claim more than one until the first works.

**Stage 3 (when ready):**
- **Consider a productized lightweight tier** — a "1-day Decide workshop" or "2-week Foundation Sprint" priced and bookable. AJ&Smart and Jake Knapp built their reputation on the productized sprint (note: the AJ&Smart Masterclass is currently waitlist-only, suggesting the format is durable enough to sell out); thoughtbot's Sprint Guide content marketing is the playbook. Use this as a top-of-funnel offering.
- **Stay opinionated about what Madrona does NOT do.** Do not become a dev shop, brand agency, or generalist consultancy. Big Medium's restraint is an enormous part of its market position.

### What Madrona should NOT borrow from larger firms

- **Don't copy IDEO/frog phase prose** ("inspiration → ideation"). It reads as 2014 design-thinking boilerplate and prospects parse it as generic.
- **Don't copy Accenture Song / fjord / Method enterprise framings** — DesignOps, "transform at scale," "operating model" language. These are signals that you are not a senior small studio; they put you in an RFP bake-off you cannot win on price.
- **Don't copy MetaLab's opacity.** Metalab refuses to share pricing and clients note this is friction. A small senior studio competes by being more transparent than the giants, not less.
- **Don't try to publish 50 case studies like Ueno or Athletics.** A senior studio publishes 6–10 deeply written case studies, each with the engagement-shape headline.

### Benchmarks / thresholds that would change the recommendation

- **If <30% of inbound leads are mission-aligned 6 months after publishing the Stewardship Sprint, drop the mission-tier as a primary offering** and treat it as a top-funnel content pillar instead.
- **If average deal size after publishing price ranges drops >20%**, retreat to "starts at" language only and remove ranges.
- **If the four-package structure causes prospects to ask "which one am I?" rather than "which one do I want?", consolidate to two (Decide + Build) plus an Embedded retainer.**
- **If a single named offering — e.g., the 2-week Decide Sprint — generates more than 50% of new inquiries, consider productizing it further** (fixed price, bookable date, public agenda) rather than leaving it as a custom-quoted package.

## Caveats

- **Pricing data is patchy and largely sourced second-hand.** The frequently cited Daniel Burka $9K–$15K sprint number traces to a proto.io blog citation, not a primary Burka publication; the $200–$300/hr Ueno benchmark is from Clutch, not Ueno. Treat as directional, not authoritative.
- **Several "studios" on the list have shifted positioning.** Substantial's current homepage now describes the firm as "an applied intelligence partner for ambitious companies" focused on AI rather than as the "digital product studio" their job postings and older copy described — but the precise repositioning date is not stated on-page. Cooper was absorbed into Designit; Method into GlobalLogic; Fjord into Accenture Song; Adaptive Path into Capital One. Their historical pages are useful reference but their current sites no longer represent independent senior-studio positioning.
- **ustwo's "From X weeks" timeframes are floors, not ranges.** Real engagements vary. Treat the published numbers as marketing anchors that filter inquiries, not delivery commitments.
- **Mission-driven case studies tend to over-index on process and under-index on commercial outcomes.** Prospects for mission-aligned clients will respond to outcome-grounded language ("105% improvement in client acquisition month-over-month" — MetaLabs review on Clutch) more than to "co-design" language alone.
- **The "studio for good" lane is crowded with vague claims.** Without a structural mechanism (B Corp, 501(c)(3), published % of capacity, named sliding scale), the claim is doing more work than it can carry. Pick one mechanism before launch — Wholegrain's published commitment ("the average CO2/page view of all new websites is less than the industry average (currently 0.5 grams per page view)") is a useful template for how specific the threshold should be.
- **Madrona's senior-collaborator model is itself a positioning asset that almost no peer publishes well.** Big Medium is the closest reference, and even they only recently named the team. There's a real opportunity to be the first to clearly say: "Founder-led, with a roster of named senior collaborators we bring in by engagement type" — make the bench legible by name and discipline, the way a film studio credits department heads.
